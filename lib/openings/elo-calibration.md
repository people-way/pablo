# Elo-Based Opening Advice Calibration
## How Pablo Adjusts Coaching by Player Level

---

## Overview

Pablo's advice is NOT one-size-fits-all. The same opening position requires completely different coaching depending on the player's Elo. A 1300 needs to know "why does the bishop go there?"; a 2100 needs to know "what are the three latest novelties in this line?"

This document defines what Pablo should emphasize at each Elo bracket.

---

## Bracket 1: 1200–1400 — "Learn to Walk"

### Primary Focus
**Don't lose pieces. Develop. Castle. Basic plans.**

### What Pablo Emphasizes in Opening Coaching

1. **Development first:** Every coaching note at this level should reinforce: develop your pieces before attacking. Moving the same piece twice in the opening is a red flag.

2. **Castle early:** If a player consistently fails to castle within the first 10 moves, Pablo flags it — regardless of opening. Uncastled kings at this level get attacked and die.

3. **Don't grab pawns in the opening:** Material greed (taking offered pawns without understanding why) is the #1 killer at this level. Pablo teaches: "Is this pawn offered freely, or is it bait?"

4. **Know your opening's one-sentence goal:**
   - Italian Game: "Get your bishop to c4, castle, then push d4"
   - French Defense: "Play ...c5 to attack White's pawn chain"
   - London System: "Develop bishops before pawns close them in, then castle"

5. **Avoid complex variations:** Steer players toward Italian, London, Caro-Kann, Queen's Indian. Away from Najdorf, Grünfeld, Dragon until they can explain WHY each move is played.

### Red Flags Pablo Watches For
- Queen developed before minor pieces
- Castling not happening by move 12
- Knight moved to the edge (a3, h3, a6, h6) without a specific reason
- Same piece moved 3+ times in the opening

### Opening Recommendations for This Bracket
**White:** London System, Colle System, Basic Italian
**Black vs 1.e4:** Caro-Kann, Basic French
**Black vs 1.d4:** London mirror (QGD), Basic Queen's Indian

### Sample Pablo Message (1300 Level)
> "You're moving the same knight three times in the first eight moves. I get it — you're reacting to threats. But here's a rule that will help you right now: develop a new piece every move unless you absolutely can't. If you follow that rule, you'll be better than 80% of players at your level just from that alone."

## Bracket 2: 1400–1600 — "Learn the Map"

### Primary Focus
**Pawn structure. Knowing your opening's strategic goal. Understanding why moves work.**

### What Pablo Emphasizes

1. **Pawn structure determines the plan:** Pablo introduces the idea that every opening creates a specific pawn structure, and that structure determines what you do for the next 30 moves. Spanish bishop vs French bishop. Isolated queen pawn. Backward pawns.

2. **Know your opening's strategic goal explicitly:** Not just "develop and castle" but:
   - Ruy Lopez: "Put pressure on e5 through the bishop on a4, prepare d4"
   - Sicilian Dragon: "The Dragon bishop on g7 controls the long diagonal — protect it, use it"
   - KID: "Push ...f5, aim for ...f4, attack the king"

3. **Recognize basic tactical patterns in YOUR opening:** Forks, pins, skewers that are specific to their opening. The Fried Liver Attack, Noah's Ark Trap, Legal's Mate — not as tricks to memorize but as patterns to recognize.

4. **Understand weak squares:** What square becomes weak after the pawns are placed? This is the beginning of positional thinking.

5. **Choose 2-3 openings and stick to them:** Pablo discourages repertoire jumping. Pick one main White opening and one Black response to each of 1.e4 and 1.d4. Build on that.

### Red Flags Pablo Watches For
- Repertoire inconsistency (playing 5 different openings as Black)
- Not knowing what to do after the first 6 moves of their own opening
- Avoiding endgames through bad trades
- Tactical patterns missed repeatedly in the same opening

### Opening Recommendations for This Bracket
**White:** Italian Game (Giuoco Piano), London System with a clear plan
**Black vs 1.e4:** Caro-Kann Classical, French Advance
**Black vs 1.d4:** Nimzo-Indian or QGD TMB variation

### Sample Pablo Message (1500 Level)
> "You're playing the French and you've been to the Advance Variation 12 times this month. Eleven of those games, you played ...Nc6 on move 4 before pushing ...c5. That's backwards. The pawn structure is screaming at you: ...c5 attacks the base of White's chain. ...Nc6 just develops a piece. Fix the order."

## Bracket 3: 1600–1800 — "The Hard Work"

### Primary Focus
**Concrete plans. Critical moves. Avoiding known traps. Move-order awareness.**

### What Pablo Emphasizes

1. **Memorize the critical moves in your opening:** Every opening has 2-3 moves that define it. In the Ruy Lopez, it's 9.h3. In the Najdorf, it's the choice between 6.Bg5 and 6.Be3. At this level, players should know these and why they matter.

2. **Awareness of common traps:** Not to memorize traps to set, but to AVOID falling into them. At this level, opponents know basic trap theory. Pablo flags specific traps the player has been caught in.

3. **Move-order matters:** Introducing the concept that the same position can be reached in different move orders, some favorable, some not. Basic transpositions (Italian to Spanish, etc.)

4. **Concrete plans in the middlegame:** Not "improve your pieces" but "Knight goes f3-d2-f1-e3-d5 to reach the d5 outpost in the Ruy Lopez." Specific routes for specific pieces.

5. **Opening preparation depth:** At this level, players should know their opening to move 10-12 in the main lines. Not 40 moves — 10-12 solid moves and then play good chess.

6. **Recognize the endgame you're heading toward:** Every opening eventually reaches characteristic endgames. Ruy Lopez players should know the Ruy Lopez rook endgame. French players should know the French bishop endgame issues.

### Red Flags Pablo Watches For
- Falling into known traps repeatedly
- Not knowing the 8th/9th move in their own opening
- Playing natural-looking moves that are actually inferior (e.g., Bxc6 in the Ruy Lopez)
- Getting outplayed specifically in the pawn structure after the opening ends

### Opening Recommendations for This Bracket
**White:** Ruy Lopez (begin learning), English, Italian Giuoco Piano (deepen)
**Black vs 1.e4:** Sicilian (pick ONE: Kan, Scheveningen, or Caro-Kann)
**Black vs 1.d4:** Nimzo-Indian + Queen's Indian as a package, or KID

### Sample Pablo Message (1700 Level)
> "You've been caught in the Noah's Ark Trap twice this month — that's the one where your Ruy Lopez bishop gets trapped by Black's c and b pawns. This isn't bad luck; it's a known trap in a line you're playing. Here's the fix: when Black plays ...c5 in the Ruy Lopez, stop and ask yourself if your bishop has an escape square. If it doesn't, take action before the pawns close in. This specific trap cost you 2 rating points this week."

## Bracket 4: 1800–2000 — "Serious Preparation"

### Primary Focus
**Deep prep on 1-2 lines. Transpositions. Model games. Opponent preparation.**

### What Pablo Emphasizes

1. **Specialize deeply:** At this level, knowing "the Sicilian" is not enough. Pick ONE Sicilian variation and know it to move 20 in the main lines. Quality over quantity.

2. **Study transpositions actively:** Know the transpositional tricks in your repertoire. Can you reach your Sicilian via the Kan move order? Can your QGD transpose to the Catalan if White plays g3? These are weapons.

3. **Study model games — not for moves, but for plans:** When Kasparov plays the Najdorf, what's his plan in the middlegame? Study 5-10 games in your opening, not to memorize, but to understand the typical motifs and plans.

4. **Endgame knowledge from the opening:** Know what endgames your opening typically leads to. Ruy Lopez leads to rook endgames with a queenside majority. Caro-Kann leads to endgames with the good bishop. Know how to win these.

5. **Opening prep as opponent research:** At this level, in tournament play, you know who you're playing. Pablo should help identify what the opponent plays and focus preparation there.

6. **Novelty awareness:** Not necessarily playing novelties, but knowing where the theoretical frontier is in your lines so you know when you're on your own.

### Red Flags Pablo Watches For
- Playing the same opening in 50+ games without studying it past move 10
- Losing consistently in the middlegame transitions from their opening
- Missing transpositions that would improve their position
- Never studying model games in their own openings

### Opening Recommendations for This Bracket
**White:** Ruy Lopez (deepen), English Opening as secondary
**Black vs 1.e4:** One of Najdorf/Sicilian Dragon/Caro-Kann deeply
**Black vs 1.d4:** Nimzo-Indian fully, Grünfeld if willing to do the work

### Sample Pablo Message (1900 Level)
> "You've played the KID 80 times in the past year and I've analyzed your games. You know the setup but you don't know the Mar del Plata structure — specifically the race between White's c5 queenside push and your f5-f4 kingside attack. That race has specific rules: if White's c5 comes before your f4, you need to switch to ...Nd7-f8-e6 and defend. You're not making that switch. Find 5 Kasparov games from the 1985 World Championship in the KID — he shows exactly when to attack and when to hold."

## Bracket 5: 2000–2200 — "Professional Preparation"

### Primary Focus
**Novelties. Opponent-specific prep. Endgame transitions. Psychological weapons.**

### What Pablo Emphasizes

1. **Find and use novelties:** At this level, the theoretical frontier matters. Pablo helps identify where the theory in your line ends (often move 15-20 in sharp lines) and what improvements are possible. This requires engine analysis.

2. **Opponent-specific preparation:** Before a tournament game, study exactly what your specific opponent plays. This is standard at IM/GM level and should begin here. Pablo cross-references known opponents' openings with current preparation.

3. **Endgame mastery from the opening:** Every strong player at this level has a clear plan from the opening to the endgame. Not just "I'll develop and see what happens" but "this pawn structure leads to a rook endgame where I want to activate my king to the f-file by move 35."

4. **Psychological opening choices:** Some openings win based on opponent psychology. The Berlin Defense frustrates attacking players. The Najdorf creates complexity that time-scramble-prone opponents mishandle. The London bores aggressive players into overcomplicating. Pablo helps identify when to use these psychological weapons.

5. **Anti-preparation:** Know what your opponents will prepare against you. If you've played the Najdorf 50 times, opponents will specifically prepare anti-Najdorf lines. Consider having a secondary system (Kan, Scheveningen) to switch to.

6. **Transition fluency:** Know exactly how your opening transitions to different endgame types. Be actively comfortable in the IQP endgame if your Tarrasch goes wrong, the minor piece endgame if your Italian simplifies, etc.

### Red Flags Pablo Watches For
- Not using computer preparation for deep lines
- Playing into the same opponent-specific preparation repeatedly
- Psychological rigidity (always playing the same weapon regardless of opponent)
- Endgame weaknesses that trace back to opening choices

### Opening Recommendations for This Bracket
**White:** One deep system (Ruy Lopez or English or 1.d4 systems)
**Black vs 1.e4:** Najdorf or Berlin or Caro-Kann deeply
**Black vs 1.d4:** Grünfeld or Nimzo at top level

### Sample Pablo Message (2100 Level)
> "Your Nimzo-Indian works well except against one specific setup: the Classical 4.Qc2 system. You've faced it 8 times this year and scored 2/8. The issue is you're playing 4...0-0 which is too slow — White builds the ideal center before you can challenge it. The fix is 4...d5 immediately, or 4...Nc6 to maintain the pin. Look at Petrosian's games from the 1960s in this line — he invented the correct defensive technique and it's still the answer 60 years later."

## Cross-Bracket Principles

### Things Pablo Never Says (at ANY level)
- "Just develop your pieces" (too vague — always specify WHICH pieces and WHERE)
- "Play natural moves" (natural moves lead to natural losses)
- "Study tactics" (every coach says this — Pablo names specific tactical patterns in the player's opening)

### Things Pablo Always Says
- The specific move or plan that needs to change
- Why that change matters (cause and effect)
- One concrete resource (model game, specific line, principle)
