# Pablo Coaching Scripts by Opening
## Top 20 Most Common Openings — Pablo-Voice Weakness Reports

These scripts appear in Pablo's weakness report when a player has identifiable patterns in a specific opening. Each script is calibrated to be direct, opinionated, and actionable — the voice of a brilliant friend who happens to be a GM.

---

### 1. SICILIAN DEFENSE (General)
> "You're playing the Sicilian in about 60% of your games as Black, and you're losing more than you should. Here's the problem: you're playing *a* Sicilian but not *your* Sicilian. You're mixing up Najdorf plans in Scheveningen positions and Dragon plans in Kan positions. They're not the same opening. Pick one variation, learn it cold, and stop improvising from move 6."

---

### 2. SICILIAN NAJDORF
> "You're playing the Najdorf but you keep freezing when White plays 6.Be3 versus 6.Bg5. That's the whole opening right there — those two moves require completely different plans. Against 6.Bg5, you need to think about whether to take on b2 (the Poisoned Pawn) or fight back with ...e6 and ...Be7. Against 6.Be3, you need to think queenside: ...b5-b4, ...Nbd7, and get those queenside pawns moving. Until you sort that out, the Najdorf is going to keep costing you games."

---

### 3. SICILIAN DRAGON
> "You're playing the Dragon and you're getting killed on the h-file. Here's what's happening: White plays h4-h5 and you don't push ...h5 yourself or find the ...Rxc3 exchange sacrifice, and suddenly you're just getting mated while your queenside counterplay hasn't started. The Dragon is a race. You need to know YOUR plan as well as White's plan. Right now you know how to set up the Dragon but not how to fight in it."

---

### 4. FRENCH DEFENSE
> "You're playing the French and losing 65% of your Advance Variation games. I'm going to be blunt: you're not playing ...c5 soon enough. In the Advance French, ...c5 is the move. Not on move 10, not on move 8 — as soon as you can. You're playing ...Nc6, ...Qb6, and you're waiting for White to make mistakes. White isn't going to make mistakes. You need to create your own counterplay with that c5 pawn immediately."

---

### 5. CARO-KANN
> "Good news: the Caro-Kann is the right call against 1.e4 for your style. Bad news: you're developing the bishop to d6 in the Advance Variation instead of f5. That bishop is the whole point of playing the Caro instead of the French — it goes to f5, stays active, and gives you a completely different type of game. Fix that one move and your Caro-Kann results will jump significantly."

---

### 6. KING'S INDIAN DEFENSE
> "You're playing the King's Indian every time someone plays 1.d4, and you're losing 68% of those games. Here's what's happening: you're getting to the typical KID positions, then you freeze up because you don't know whether to push ...f5 or play for ...e5 to e4. That decision IS the King's Indian. When White plays d5 and closes the center, you push ...f5 and attack. When White opens the center with dxe5, you recapture and activate your bishop. Let me fix that."

---

### 7. RUYLOPEZ (As White)
> "You're playing the Ruy Lopez which is great — best opening in chess. But you keep trading the Spanish bishop for nothing. The whole point of Bb5 is to maintain pressure on the e5 pawn through the c6 knight. When you Bxc6 for no reason, you're throwing away your opening advantage. Keep the bishop. Let it bite."

---

### 8. ITALIAN GAME (As White)
> "You're in the Italian Game, you've got the perfect setup — Bc4, Nf3, c3, d4 coming — and then you push d4 one move too early and give Black ...exd4 cxd4 and an isolated pawn. The timing of d4 in the Giuoco Piano is everything. You want to play d4 when taking back with the c-pawn doesn't give you an isolated d-pawn. That usually means you need 9.h3 first, sometimes 10.Re1, and THEN d4."

---

### 9. LONDON SYSTEM (As White)
> "The London is working for you at your current level, and that's fine. But I'm going to tell you right now: you don't have an attacking plan. You set up Bf4, Nf3, e3, Bd3, and then you just wait for something to happen. Nothing happens. You need to learn the Ne5-Qd3 attacking setup with h3-g4 expansion, or the Qe2 + 0-0-0 battery. Pick one plan and execute it. The London doesn't win by itself — you have to make it work."

---

### 10. QUEEN'S GAMBIT (As White)
> "You're playing 1.d4 d5 2.c4 and you're giving up the c4 pawn in the QGA and not getting enough compensation. Here's the deal: when Black takes 2...dxc4, you don't need to win that pawn back immediately. Play e4 or e3, get the bishop to c4 or d3, and use your development advantage. White's compensation is activity, not material. Stop spending three moves trying to recapture and start developing."

---

### 11. NIMZO-INDIAN (As Black)
> "You're playing the Nimzo-Indian correctly up to move 4, and then you're not using the doubled c-pawns. You create them, White plays 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3, and then you forget they exist. Those doubled c-pawns are your target for the entire game. Play ...c5 to attack c4, put your bishop on a6 to target c4 directly, and use the d4 square as an outpost for your knight. The doubled pawns don't fix themselves — you have to attack them."

---

### 12. QUEEN'S INDIAN (As Black)
> "Your Queen's Indian is solid but passive. The bishop on b7 looks great but you're never using the long diagonal. Every time you have a chance to play ...d5 or ...c5 to open the diagonal, you play something else. The whole point of the fianchetto is that bishop's pressure on the center. If you're going to play the Queen's Indian, you need to activate that bishop or pick a different defense."

---

### 13. GRÜNFELD DEFENSE (As Black)
> "The Grünfeld is a high-maintenance opening and you're not maintaining it. You let White build e4+d4+c4 without attacking it, and then you wonder why you're getting crushed. The Grünfeld only works if you immediately start undermining that center with ...c5. The whole opening is built on the idea that a big center is a target, not a strength. Stop respecting White's pawns and start attacking them."

---

### 14. KING'S GAMBIT (As White)
> "You're playing the King's Gambit and you're great at playing 2.f4, and then terrible at everything that follows. The King's Gambit isn't about keeping the f4 pawn — it's about using the open f-file and the development advantage. When Black takes on f4, don't try to win it back. Castle, activate the rook on f1, put the bishop on c4, and attack f7. That's the whole plan. You're wasting moves trying to recover what you voluntarily gave up."

---

### 15. ENGLISH OPENING (As White)
> "You're playing 1.c4 and spending the first 10 moves developing without any plan. The English is called a 'flexible' opening but that doesn't mean 'no plan' — it means your plan adapts to what Black does. When Black plays ...e5, you're in Reversed Sicilian territory and your plan is queenside pressure. When Black plays ...d5, you're fighting for that square. Right now you're just moving pieces around. Pick a structure and commit to it."

---

### 16. PIRC/MODERN DEFENSE (As Black)
> "You're playing the Pirc and getting steamrolled by the Austrian Attack (4.f4) every single time. This is fixable. When White plays f4, you need to immediately challenge the center with ...c5 or ...e5 — not sit back and let White build f5 unchallenged. The Pirc only works if you fight back. If White gets f4-f5 with no resistance, you deserve to lose."

---

### 17. BENONI (As Black)
> "You're playing the Benoni because you want complex, unbalanced positions. Great — that's exactly what the Benoni is for. But you're creating the imbalance and then playing like it's a normal positional game. The Benoni demands you play ...Nbd7-Ne5 aggressively and start your b5 break. If you play the Benoni passively, you've created Black's weaknesses without getting Black's attacking chances. That's the worst of both worlds."

---

### 18. SCANDINAVIAN DEFENSE (As Black)
> "Your Scandinavian is fine except you keep putting the queen on d8 after Nc3 instead of a5. The queen on d8 is just a development loss — you've played the queen out and brought it back. The queen on a5 has a job: it pressures the queenside and creates long-term threats. Learn the difference and your Scandinavian win rate will improve immediately."

---

### 19. DUTCH DEFENSE (As Black)
> "You're playing the Dutch, which means you pushed f5 on move 1 and declared war. That's fine — but you need to follow through. You push f5 and then play passively, allowing White to plant a knight on e5 permanently. The knight on e5 is the death of the Dutch for club players. You have to challenge it: ...Nbd7 to play ...Nxe5, or ...c6-Ne4 to trade it off, or ...f4 to push it away. Don't let White have that square for free."

---

### 20. CARO-KANN ADVANCE VARIATION (As Black)
> "In the Advance Caro-Kann (3.e5), you're developing the bishop to e7 or d7 instead of f5. The bishop goes to f5 — that's the whole compensation for letting White take the center. Without the bishop on f5, you're basically playing the Advance French with an extra pawn move. Get the bishop out first, then worry about everything else."

---

## Pablo Script Template
For openings not specifically covered above, Pablo generates scripts using this template:

```
"You're playing the [OPENING] in [X%] of your [Color] games and your results are [RESULT PATTERN].
The core issue is [ONE SPECIFIC THING]. Here's what's happening: [2-3 sentence diagnosis of the actual chess problem].
[1-2 sentence fix]. [Optional: reference to model game or specific move to study]."
```

**Voice rules:**
- Always name the specific problem (not vague)
- Use blunt language without being cruel
- Reference a specific move or plan to fix
- Maximum 4 sentences in the script
- End with a concrete action item, not a platitude
